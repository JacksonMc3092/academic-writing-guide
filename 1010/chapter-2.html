Chapter 2: Annotating Your Way to Greatness

Making Your Reading Visible and Useful

Course Hub

In Chapter 1, active reading was presented as a purposeful way of engaging with written, visual, spoken, and digital texts. Annotation is one of the most useful ways to leave a record of that engagement.

When you annotate, you do more than highlight. You mark important passages, write questions, note connections, define terms, identify patterns, and explain why a passage matters. These notes make your thinking visible so that you can return to the text later without beginning again from zero.

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

  • explain the difference between highlighting and annotation;
  • identify useful purposes for annotation;
  • annotate in print, digital, and borrowed-text situations;
  • write margin notes that summarize, question, connect, analyze, and plan;
  • use a flexible annotation process for both accessible and difficult texts;
  • turn annotations into material for summary, discussion, research, and drafting.

Active reading happens in the mind. Annotation gives that thinking a place to live.

A highlighted sentence may tell you that something looked important at the time. A useful annotation tells you why it mattered.

Consider the difference:

Highlight only:

Students who sleep fewer than seven hours often report greater difficulty concentrating in morning classes.

Highlight plus annotation:

Possible evidence for my paragraph on late-night phone use. I need to check whether the study distinguishes correlation from causation.

The second version preserves more than a sentence. It records a possible use, a question about the evidence, and a connection to the writer’s developing project.

Key Insight: Highlighting marks a passage. Annotation records your thinking about the passage.

Annotation can be brief. A question mark, a five-word summary, or a note such as “contradicts the earlier claim” may be enough. The goal is not to cover every inch of the page with commentary. The goal is to leave a useful trail.

Annotation supports several parts of the reading and writing process.

Keep you engaged

Writing a question, summary, or reaction requires you to pause and make a decision about what you have read. That small act interrupts passive reading.

Check comprehension

A short summary in the margin reveals whether you understand the paragraph or section. If you cannot restate the idea, you may need to slow down, reread with a purpose, or identify an unfamiliar term.

Preserve questions

Questions that occur while reading are easy to lose. Writing them beside the relevant passage helps you remember where the uncertainty began.

Identify useful evidence

Annotations can mark definitions, examples, data, quotations, or patterns that may matter in a later assignment.

Prepare for discussion

A page containing questions, agreements, disagreements, and connections gives you material to bring into class or peer discussion.

Build toward writing

Annotations often become summary notes, paragraph ideas, evidence choices, research questions, or outline points.

Key Insight: Annotation is a bridge between reading and writing because it stores the ideas, questions, and evidence that drafting will later need.

Annotation across disciplines

The focus of annotation changes with the task and field.

Context What you might notice Possible notes
Literature and humanities themes, symbols, character choices, imagery, language, patterns “Repeated image,” “change in tone,” “why this word?”
Sciences methods, variables, data, findings, definitions, limits “Key result,” “sample size?” “cause or correlation?”
Social sciences claims, evidence, assumptions, methods, groups, consequences “Whose perspective?” “strong evidence,” “missing group”
Professional or technical reading procedures, requirements, exceptions, risks, definitions “Action step,” “deadline,” “exception,” “ask supervisor”
Visual and digital texts design, editing, sequence, labels, sound, source, emphasis “Music shapes mood,” “scale begins at 50,” “source missing”

The symbols are optional. The thinking is not.

A page becomes useful when your marks and comments have a clear purpose.

Mark selectively

Consider marking:

  • the central claim or main idea;
  • key supporting claims;
  • important definitions;
  • strong or questionable evidence;
  • useful examples;
  • important qualifications or exceptions;
  • repeated ideas or patterns;
  • passages whose exact wording may deserve quotation;
  • confusing or unfamiliar sections;
  • material connected to your assignment or research question.

If nearly every sentence is highlighted, the page has become a yellow weather event rather than a reading tool.

Write different kinds of margin notes

Summarize

State the paragraph or section’s main point in your own words.

Main idea: the writer links sleep loss to reduced attention, not lower intelligence.

Question

Identify something unclear, unsupported, incomplete, or worth investigating.

Does this study include commuter students?

Connect

Link the passage to another text, course concept, experience, or public issue.

Connects to Wallace’s idea about automatic habits.

Analyze

Notice how the writer’s choices shape meaning.

The example appears before the data, perhaps to make the issue feel personal first.

Evaluate

Assess the strength, limits, or usefulness of the material.

Useful statistic, but the source is ten years old.

Plan

Record how you might use the passage later.

Possible evidence for body paragraph 2. Pair with the campus survey.

Define

Clarify an unfamiliar or specialized term.

“Mediation” here means the effect operates through another factor.

A short code can help when you are working quickly, but the meaning should remain clear when you return days or weeks later.

There is no single correct annotation system. Choose tools that fit the text, assignment, access needs, and ownership of the material.

Printed texts you own

You may use:

  • pencil or pen;
  • underlining and circling;
  • brief margin notes;
  • sticky flags;
  • arrows connecting related passages;
  • short summaries at the end of sections.

Borrowed books or library materials

Do not write in materials you do not own.

Instead, use:

  • removable sticky notes or flags;
  • a separate reading journal;
  • a page-numbered note sheet;
  • photographs or scans when permitted;
  • a two-column system with passage information on one side and your response on the other.

PDFs and e-books

Digital annotation tools may allow you to:

  • highlight;
  • attach comments;
  • draw or underline;
  • search for terms;
  • add bookmarks;
  • copy citation information;
  • export notes.

Highlighting alone is still limited. Add a comment that records the purpose of the mark.

Videos, podcasts, and speeches

You cannot write in the margin of a video, but you can create the equivalent.

Record:

  • timestamps;
  • the central claim;
  • key evidence or examples;
  • changes in tone or direction;
  • important images, sounds, or editing choices;
  • questions or points to verify;
  • brief summaries of major segments.

Images, maps, and infographics

Use arrows, labels, or numbered notes to identify:

  • what is emphasized;
  • what is omitted;
  • how color, scale, position, or perspective guides interpretation;
  • where the information came from;
  • what requires verification.

Key Insight: The best annotation tool is the one that leaves you with readable, retrievable notes that help you understand and use the text later.

The current Scholar’s Compass chapter used a three-pass process. That structure remains useful, but it should be flexible rather than ceremonial.

Pass 1: Preview and orient

Before reading closely:

  • identify the author, title, publication, and date;
  • scan headings, visuals, and organization;
  • note the assignment or purpose;
  • predict the central subject or question;
  • identify what kind of attention the text will require.

You do not always need to read the entire text before marking anything. The point of the first pass is orientation.

Pass 2: Read and respond

As you read:

  • mark key claims and evidence;
  • write questions;
  • summarize difficult sections;
  • identify terms;
  • connect ideas;
  • notice rhetorical choices;
  • record possible uses.

For every major highlight, ask: Why am I marking this?

Pass 3: Review and carry forward

After reading:

  • review your notes;
  • write a one- or two-sentence summary of the text;
  • identify the most important annotation;
  • note one remaining question;
  • mark two or three passages worth revisiting;
  • record how the text may contribute to an assignment, discussion, or research project.

This final review turns scattered notes into a usable record.

A quick annotation checklist

  • [ ] I identified my purpose before reading closely.
  • [ ] I marked selectively rather than highlighting everything.
  • [ ] My notes explain why important passages matter.
  • [ ] I included at least one question.
  • [ ] I summarized at least one difficult section.
  • [ ] I recorded at least one connection or possible use.
  • [ ] I reviewed my annotations after finishing.

The following original Scholar’s Compass practice paragraph is designed to show how several annotation moves can work together without relying on an invented outside source.

Practice paragraph

Many college students describe short-form video as a way to relax between tasks. Because the videos are brief, users may assume that checking a feed will take only a minute. However, autoplay, personalized recommendations, and the absence of a clear stopping point can turn a short break into a much longer session. The problem may not be the existence of the platform alone, but the interaction between its design and the routines users develop around it.

Possible annotations

  1. Main idea: The writer argues that platform design and user routine work together.
  2. Question: What evidence shows that autoplay lengthens sessions?
  3. Key distinction: The claim is not simply “short-form video is bad.”
  4. Connection: Relates to habits and designed environments.
  5. Analysis: The phrase “absence of a clear stopping point” identifies a design feature rather than blaming only the user.
  6. Possible use: Could support a paragraph about how platforms shape attention.

From annotation to summary

Using those notes, a student might write:

The paragraph explains that short-form video sessions can last longer than users expect because platform features such as autoplay and personalized recommendations interact with established viewing habits.

From annotation to analysis

A student might also develop this observation:

By shifting attention from individual self-control to the interaction between design and routine, the paragraph complicates the common assumption that extended scrolling results only from poor personal choices.

The annotations support both summary and analysis, but the two moves are different. Summary explains what the passage says. Analysis explains how the idea works or why it matters.

Chapter connection: Chapter 3 develops summary in greater detail. Later chapters return to analysis, evidence, argument, and synthesis.

Dense readings require more support, not more random highlighting.

Use paragraph summaries

Write a five- to ten-word summary beside each difficult paragraph.

Examples:

  • defines the central problem;
  • gives historical background;
  • introduces competing explanation;
  • presents evidence;
  • qualifies the earlier claim.

Break long sentences into parts

Identify:

  1. the main subject;
  2. the main verb;
  3. the central claim;
  4. qualifying phrases;
  5. examples or evidence.

Track key terms

Circle or list repeated terms. Note whether the writer defines them consistently or shifts their meaning.

Write the confusion precisely

Instead of writing “I don’t get it,” identify the problem:

  • unfamiliar term;
  • unclear pronoun;
  • missing connection;
  • complicated sentence structure;
  • unstated assumption;
  • unfamiliar context.

A precise question is easier to solve.

Use the dialogue method

Write brief responses as though you were speaking to the writer:

  • What evidence supports this?
  • I follow the first point, but not the conclusion.
  • This example clarifies the definition.
  • How would this apply in a different setting?
  • This seems to conflict with the earlier section.

Know when to pause

A difficult passage may require a dictionary, course notes, another source, or a question for the instructor. Annotation helps you identify where outside help is needed.

Practice 1: The one-page challenge

Choose one page of an assigned reading. Add at least four different kinds of annotations:

  • one summary;
  • one question;
  • one connection;
  • one possible use or analysis note.

Then identify which annotation will be most useful later and explain why.

Practice 2: Annotation makeover

Return to a reading you previously highlighted without adding notes. For five highlighted passages, add a comment explaining:

  1. why the passage matters;
  2. what question it raises;
  3. how you might use or respond to it.

Practice 3: From annotation to summary

Annotate a short reading, then write a two- or three-sentence summary using only your notes. Compare the summary with the original text and correct anything inaccurate or incomplete.

Practice 4: Across media

Choose a short video, podcast segment, infographic, or map. Create an annotation log using timestamps, labels, arrows, or numbered notes. Include:

  • the main message;
  • one important choice in presentation;
  • one piece of evidence;
  • one question;
  • one item to verify.

Practice 5: From annotation to outline

Choose a source connected to a current assignment. Review your annotations and sort them into possible roles:

  • background;
  • definition;
  • evidence;
  • counterpoint;
  • example;
  • limitation;
  • question for further research.

Use those roles to sketch one possible paragraph or outline section.

Annotation leaves you with pieces: questions, definitions, notes, reactions, and marked passages. The next step is to determine which ideas are central and represent them accurately in your own words.

Before moving forward, choose one annotated reading and complete this brief handoff:

  1. State the text’s main idea in one sentence.
  2. Identify two supporting points.
  3. Name one detail that is interesting but not central.
  4. Write one question that remains.
  5. Identify one passage you may need to quote, paraphrase, or revisit.

In Chapter 3: Summarizing Your Way to Synthesis, you will learn how to turn that reading record into an accurate, focused summary.

You are ready to continue when you can:

  • explain why highlighting alone is limited;
  • identify at least five useful annotation moves;
  • annotate in print, digital, borrowed, and audiovisual formats;
  • use a flexible preview-read-review process;
  • write margin notes that remain understandable later;
  • turn annotations into a summary, question, or writing plan.
