Chapter 6

Finding and Evaluating Sources

Research is not “collecting quotes.” It’s finding the best conversation partners for your question — and keeping track of what you find so you can use it responsibly.

Course Hub

I. Introduction: Why research exists

Research exists because none of us can know everything firsthand. Academic writing asks you to build a claim that can survive contact with reality — evidence, context, and other informed viewpoints. A strong source is not just “credible.” It’s also useful for the specific job your paper is doing.

Your goal in this chapter
  • Start with a working question (not a vague topic).
  • Search in smart places (not just one place).
  • Evaluate quickly and realistically using SIFT (Stop, Investigate, Find better coverage, Trace).
  • Track what you find using a source log.
  • Begin MLA basics now — then deepen in Chapter 7 and Chapter 8.
Bridge to Chapter 9 (Topic → Thesis)

Think of your working question as your steering wheel. It keeps your research from turning into random browsing. Later, when you move into the topic → working thesis work in Chapter 9, your question becomes the fuel for a claim: what you learned, what’s at stake, and what you want your reader to believe. (See Chapter 9: Crafting Powerful Thesis Statements.)

II. Starting Right: Question first, then keywords

1) Turn a topic into a working question

A topic is a territory. A working question is a route. It tells you what to look for and how to decide whether a source actually helps.

Topic → Working Question examples
  • Topic: Social media and mental health
    Working question: How does heavy social media use affect anxiety in first-year college students, and what interventions show evidence of helping?
  • Topic: AI in school writing
    Working question: When students use generative AI for drafting, what changes in learning outcomes are documented, and what policies reduce harm without banning tools outright?
  • Topic: Food deserts
    Working question: Which solutions measurably improve fresh-food access: new grocery stores, mobile markets, or transportation support — and under what conditions?

2) Build keyword “families” (not one magic phrase)

Most students search like this: one phrase, one time, hope for miracles. Instead, build keyword families you can swap in and out.

Keyword family template
  • Core terms: the main topic words (e.g., “social media,” “anxiety”)
  • Synonyms: alternate words (e.g., “Instagram,” “stress,” “mental health”)
  • Academic terms: discipline language (e.g., “problematic use,” “generalized anxiety disorder”)
  • Limiters: population/place/time (e.g., “first-year,” “college,” “United States,” “2020–2026”)

Tip: your keywords get better as you read. Research is allowed to be iterative. (That’s the point.)

III. Where to Search: Three lanes that work

You’re not choosing between “library” and “Google.” You’re choosing the right lane for the job. Most strong research blends all three.

Lane 1: Library databases (best for peer-reviewed scholarship)

  • Use when you need: scholarly articles, academic books/chapters, credible reports.
  • Best move: start broad, then narrow using subject terms, filters, and date ranges.
  • Reality check: you may need 1–2 scholarly sources plus other credible sources (policy, journalism, data) depending on your assignment.

Lane 2: Google Scholar (best for citation trails)

Google Scholar is powerful because it shows you a network: newer work that cites something, related work, and multiple versions. Scholar itself recommends using “Cited by,” “Related articles,” and “All versions” to keep exploring efficiently (Google Scholar Search Help).

  • “Cited by” = newer work that references the item you found.
  • “Related articles” = similar work (a fast way to expand).
  • “All versions” = alternate locations, sometimes including free PDFs.
  • Library links = connect to your college subscriptions (if configured).
Important warning about auto-generated citations

Tools that generate citations (including Scholar’s “Cite” button) are helpful, but not perfect. Use them as a draft, then check your details against MLA rules (MLA Style Center; Purdue OWL).

Lane 3: The open web (best for context, policy, and credible public sources)

  • Use when you need: government data, nonprofit reports, reputable journalism, organizational statements, primary documents.
  • Best move: evaluate quickly using lateral reading/SIFT before you commit time to close reading.

IV. Evaluating Sources: Credible isn’t enough — it must also be useful

1) Use SIFT for a fast credibility check

SIFT is designed to help you evaluate sources quickly and avoid getting trapped by polished design or confident tone. The Center for an Informed Public describes SIFT as a four-step method developed by Mike Caulfield: Stop, Investigate, Find better coverage, and Trace claims/media back to the original (Center for an Informed Public).

SIFT in student-language
  • Stop: Pause when you feel pulled in. Strong emotion is a signal to slow down.
  • Investigate the source: Who are they? What’s their mission? What do reliable places say about them?
  • Find better coverage: Is this claim supported by more trustworthy outlets or research?
  • Trace: Follow the claim to the original study, document, quote, or dataset.

2) Why “lateral reading” beats close reading at the start

When people evaluate a source by staying on the page (scrolling, checking the About page, admiring the logo), they often get fooled. Research on expert fact-checkers shows they “read laterally”: they leave the site quickly and open new tabs to check what other sources say (Wineburg and McGrew; Caulfield).

3) The usefulness check (with a concrete example)

A source can be credible and still be wrong for your paper. Before you adopt a source, run four quick questions:

Usefulness check
  • Fit: Does it actually address my working question?
  • Level: Is it too general or too specialized for what I need right now?
  • Role: What job will it do in my paper (background, evidence, counterargument, definition, method, example)?
  • Conversation: Does it help me join a real discussion (not just repeat a point)? (ACRL Framework)
Mini example (usefulness in action)

Let’s say your working question is: “How does social media use affect anxiety in first-year college students?”

  • You find a credible article about teen anxiety and TikTok. It’s interesting, but your population is different. That might become background context, not your core evidence.
  • You find a peer-reviewed study on college students and anxiety that includes a clear method and findings. That’s a strong candidate for evidence.
  • You find an opinion piece from a tech CEO. It may be relevant as a stakeholder viewpoint, but it isn’t research evidence.

Same topic, different jobs. Usefulness is about rhetorical purpose, not vibes.

V. Managing & Citing: Source logs + MLA basics (with a preview of Chapter 7)

1) Use a source log so your research doesn’t vanish

A source log is a simple system for tracking what you found, why it matters, and how you’ll use it. It keeps you from “PDF hoarding” and makes drafting faster.

Source log template (copy/paste and reuse)
Pro tip: fill this out while you search, not after you forget everything.
Full citation (MLA draft) Lane Credibility notes (SIFT) Usefulness notes Key passage / data How I’ll use this
[Paste your draft Works Cited entry here] Database / Scholar / Web Who published it? What do other sources say? Original source traced? Answers my question? What part? Any limitations? Quote, statistic, or finding (include page/section if available) Background / Evidence / Counter / Definition / Example

2) MLA basics: in-text citations + Works Cited

MLA is not just formatting. It’s how your reader sees your evidence trail. The basic rule is: when you use someone else’s words or ideas, you cite them in the sentence and in your Works Cited. Purdue OWL describes MLA as an author-page in-text citation system (Purdue OWL).

In-text citation basics (MLA)
  • If you name the author in the sentence, put the page number in parentheses:
    Smith argues that … (42).
  • If you don’t name the author in the sentence, include the author and page:
    … in college writing (Smith 42).
  • If there’s no page number (common on web pages), MLA has alternate approaches — your instructor will tell you what to prioritize. (Start with MLA Style Center.)

VI. Practice: A complete research sprint (one workflow, start to finish)

This practice is designed to feel like real research: a short, focused sprint that produces a usable set of sources and a clean record. Use a timer if that helps. Your future self will thank you.

Research Sprint (45–60 minutes)
  1. Write a working question (1–2 sentences). Make it specific enough to guide searching.
  2. Create two keyword families (core terms + synonyms + academic terms).
  3. Find one source in each lane:
    • 1 scholarly source (database)
    • 1 scholarly source (Google Scholar)
    • 1 credible public source (open web: gov/nonprofit/reputable journalism)
  4. Run SIFT on the open web source (at minimum), and any source that feels questionable.
  5. Fill out your source log for all 3 sources.
  6. Write 2 sentences of synthesis:
    • Sentence 1: what your sources agree on (or what seems most established).
    • Sentence 2: where they differ, or what question remains unresolved.
  7. Draft one MLA in-text citation and one Works Cited entry (draft is fine).
Exit ticket (fast reflection)
  • What keyword change improved my results most?
  • Which lane gave me the most useful source — and why?
  • What claim am I currently leaning toward (a possible working thesis)?

Checklist

  • I can state a working question that guides my search.
  • I can generate keyword families and revise them as I learn.
  • I can use databases, Google Scholar, and the open web strategically.
  • I can use SIFT and lateral reading to evaluate credibility.
  • I can explain why a source is useful for my purpose (not just “credible”).
  • I can keep a source log that supports drafting and citation.
  • I can produce a draft MLA in-text citation and Works Cited entry.
