I. Introduction: Why research exists

Research is how you join a conversation you didn’t start. It helps you move from a general topic to a focused claim with real evidence behind it.

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  • Turn a topic into a workable research question
  • Search efficiently in databases, Google Scholar, and the open web
  • Evaluate credibility quickly using the SIFT method
  • Decide whether a source is useful for your purpose (not just “credible”)
  • Track sources with a source log and begin MLA citation basics

Key Takeaway

Good research is not “collecting quotes.” It is choosing strong conversation partners for your question — and keeping an evidence trail your reader can follow.

Bridge to Chapter 9 (Topic → Thesis): Think of your working question as your steering wheel. It keeps research from turning into random browsing. When you move into the topic → working thesis work in Chapter 9, what you discover here becomes the foundation for a claim: what’s at stake, what patterns you see, and what you want your reader to accept. (See Chapter 9: Crafting Powerful Thesis Statements.)

II. Starting Right: Question first, then keywords

1) Turn a topic into a working question

A topic is a territory. A working question is a route. It tells you what to look for, what to ignore, and what “good evidence” would even count as.

Topic → Working question examples

Topic: Social media and mental health
Working question: How does heavy social media use affect anxiety in first-year college students, and what interventions show evidence of helping?

Topic: AI in school writing
Working question: When students use generative AI for drafting, what changes in learning outcomes are documented, and what policies reduce harm without banning tools outright?

Topic: Food deserts
Working question: Which solutions measurably improve fresh-food access: new grocery stores, mobile markets, or transportation support — and under what conditions?

2) Build keyword “families” (not one perfect search phrase)

Most students search once, get weak results, and assume the topic is “hard.” Better move: build keyword families you can swap in and out as you learn.

Start by identifying the 2–3 main concepts in your working question. Then build a small family of terms for each concept. This gives you control: you can broaden, narrow, or shift vocabulary without changing your topic.

Keyword family checklist

  • Core terms: your everyday words for the concept
  • Synonyms: alternate words people might use
  • Academic terms: discipline language (often learned from abstracts and subject terms)
  • Narrowers: specific versions (population, setting, time frame, subtopic)
  • Expanders: broader related terms (useful when results are too limited)
  • Counter-terms: language used by people who frame the issue differently

Tip: your keywords should improve as you read. That’s not failure — it’s the process working.

Example: keyword families from one working question

Working question: How does heavy social media use affect anxiety in first-year college students?

Concept A: Social media use
Core terms: social media, Instagram, TikTok, Snapchat
Academic terms: social media engagement, problematic use, passive consumption
Narrowers: late-night use, notification frequency, doomscrolling

Concept B: Anxiety / mental health
Core terms: anxiety, stress, mental health
Academic terms: psychological distress, well-being, symptoms
Narrowers: sleep disruption, social anxiety, test anxiety

Concept C: First-year college students
Core terms: first-year students, freshmen, new students
Academic terms: transition to college, college adjustment, transition stress
Narrowers: commuter students, first-generation students, residential students

Search logic that works: OR expands, AND focuses

Use OR inside one concept (synonyms), and AND between concepts. That’s how you control your results.

Search logic examples

("social media" OR Instagram OR TikTok OR "social networking")
AND (anxiety OR "psychological distress" OR well-being)
AND ("first-year" OR freshman OR "college transition")

Two quick keyword upgrades that make research better

  • Upgrade 1: add academic language. Scholarship often uses different terms than everyday conversation. If you only search everyday terms, you may miss stronger research.
  • Upgrade 2: separate keywords from filters. Don’t type “peer reviewed” into the search bar. Use database filters (peer-reviewed checkbox, date range, subject terms). Keywords are for meaning; filters narrow the kind of material you’re seeing.

Where the best keywords come from: once you find one strong source, “mine” it for language. Look at the abstract, headings, and subject terms. Those words often become your next best search terms.

Keyword pivot plan (when you feel stuck)

  • Too many results? Add a narrower (population, place, time frame) or swap a broad term for an academic term.
  • Too few results? remove a narrower, add an expander, or try broader synonyms for one concept.

Your goal is not one perfect search. Your goal is a set of searches you can revise on purpose.

Keyword Builder: 6-minute method

  1. Circle the 2–3 concepts in your working question.
  2. Write 3–5 core terms for each concept.
  3. Add 2–3 synonyms and 1–2 academic terms for each concept.
  4. Build 2 searches using OR (within concepts) + AND (between concepts).
  5. Run the searches, then revise one concept at a time.
III. Where to Search: Three lanes that work

You’re not choosing between “library” and “Google.” You’re choosing the right lane for the job. Strong research often uses all three.

The three-lane search model

  • Lane 1: Library databases (best for peer-reviewed scholarship and academic books)
  • Lane 2: Google Scholar (best for citation trails: “Cited by,” “Related articles,” and “All versions”)
  • Lane 3: The open web (best for government data, reputable journalism, nonprofit reports, primary documents)

Lane 1: Library databases

  • Start broad, then narrow using filters (date range, peer-reviewed, subject terms).
  • Use abstracts to decide quickly: keep / maybe / no.
  • Save the strongest items immediately (PDF + citation info + link).

Lane 2: Google Scholar (use it like a map)

Google Scholar’s own help page encourages using features like citation links and alerts to get better results and track new work (see Google Scholar Search Help).

  • Cited by = newer work that references the item you found
  • Related articles = a fast way to widen the conversation
  • All versions = alternate locations (sometimes including free PDFs)
  • Cite = a starting point for citations (use as a draft; always check details)

Fast reality check: Google Scholar is powerful, but it includes many kinds of materials. Your job is to evaluate what you found — not assume it’s automatically scholarly.

Lane 3: The open web

  • Good targets: .gov, reputable nonprofits, established news outlets, policy organizations, major institutions.
  • Use a quick evaluation method (SIFT) before you invest time in close reading.
IV. Evaluating Sources: Credible isn’t enough — it must be useful

1) Use SIFT for a fast credibility check

SIFT is a practical method developed by Mike Caulfield to help readers verify online information quickly: Stop, Investigate the source, Find better coverage, and Trace claims back to the original context.

Learn SIFT from: UW Center for an Informed Public and University of Chicago Library guide.

SIFT in student-language

  • Stop: Pause. If you feel instantly convinced or outraged, slow down.
  • Investigate: Who is behind this? What’s their track record?
  • Find better coverage: Are reliable sources confirming this claim?
  • Trace: Follow the claim to the original study, document, quote, or dataset.

2) Why “lateral reading” works

Instead of staying on one page and judging by design, experts often leave the page to see what trusted sources say elsewhere. Wineburg and McGrew describe this as lateral reading: opening new tabs to investigate a source’s credibility and context.

3) The usefulness check (separate from credibility)

A source can be credible and still be wrong for your paper. Before you commit, ask:

  • Fit: Does it address my working question?
  • Level: Is it too general or too specialized for what I need right now?
  • Role: What job will it do (background, evidence, counterargument, definition, example, method)?
  • Conversation: Does it help me enter an ongoing discussion rather than repeat obvious points? (See the ACRL Framework.)
Mini example: usefulness in action

Working question: How does heavy social media use affect anxiety in first-year college students?

Source A: A credible article about teen anxiety and TikTok. Useful as background, but the population may not match your question.
Source B: A peer-reviewed study on college students and anxiety with clear methods and findings. Strong candidate for core evidence.
Source C: An opinion piece from a tech executive. Useful as a stakeholder viewpoint, not as research evidence.
V. Managing & Citing: Source logs + MLA basics (preview)

1) Use a source log so your research doesn’t vanish

A source log is a simple system for tracking what you found, why it matters, and how you’ll use it. It keeps your drafting process fast and your citations accurate.

Source log template (copy and reuse)

Fill this out while you search. Don’t wait until the night before the draft.

[Paste your draft citation] Database / Scholar / Web Who is behind it? Better coverage? Original traced? Answers which part of my question? Any limits? Quote, statistic, or finding (page/section if available) Background / Evidence / Counter / Definition / Example

2) MLA basics (starter version)

MLA is an author-page citation system: you cite sources in the sentence and list full details on the Works Cited page (see Purdue OWL and MLA Style Center).

  • If you name the author in your sentence, include the page number in parentheses: Smith argues that… (42).
  • If you do not name the author in the sentence: …in college writing (Smith 42).
  • If there is no page number (common on web pages), your instructor will show you the preferred approach for your course (MLA Style Center has guidance).

What happens next: Chapter 7 teaches quoting, paraphrasing, and signal phrases (the “sentence-level” integration moves). Chapter 8 teaches how to use sources rhetorically and synthesize them into your argument.

VI. Practice: One research sprint (start to finish)

This practice is designed to feel like real research: a focused sprint that produces usable sources and a clean record.

Research Sprint (45–60 minutes)
  1. Write a working question (1–2 sentences).
  2. Create two keyword families (core + synonyms + academic terms + limiters).
  3. Find one source in each lane:
    • 1 scholarly source (database)
    • 1 scholarly source (Google Scholar)
    • 1 credible public source (open web)
  4. Run SIFT on the open web source (minimum) and any source that feels questionable.
  5. Complete your source log for all three sources.
  6. Write 2 sentences of synthesis:
    • Sentence 1: what your sources agree on (or what seems most established).
    • Sentence 2: where they differ, or what remains unresolved.
  7. Draft one MLA in-text citation and one MLA Works Cited entry.
  8. Optional bridge to Chapter 9: write a one-sentence working thesis based on what your strongest sources suggest so far.

Exit Ticket

  • What keyword change improved your results most?
  • Which lane produced your most useful source — and why?
  • What claim are you leaning toward (a possible working thesis)?
