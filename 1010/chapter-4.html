Chapter 4: Quoting, Paraphrasing, and Signal Phrases

Choosing, Integrating, and Citing Source Material in MLA Style

Course Hub

Chapter 3 focused on summary: representing the central ideas of a larger source accurately and concisely. This chapter turns to two other ways of using source material. A quotation preserves a source’s exact words. A paraphrase restates a specific passage in new language and sentence structure while preserving its meaning and level of detail.

Neither method is automatically stronger. The best choice depends on what your paragraph needs. Sometimes the exact wording matters. Sometimes the idea matters more than the wording. In both cases, the source must be introduced clearly, cited accurately, and connected to your own purpose.

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

  • choose appropriately among summary, paraphrase, and quotation;
  • identify clear reasons to preserve a source’s exact wording;
  • quote accurately and integrate short quotations into your own sentences;
  • paraphrase a passage using genuinely new wording and sentence structure;
  • use signal phrases and signal verbs to establish source boundaries;
  • apply MLA in-text citation patterns to paginated, unpaginated, and audiovisual sources;
  • use the quote-sandwich method as a flexible structure for introducing and explaining evidence;
  • avoid dropped quotations, patchwriting, citation duplication, and other common source-integration problems.

Summary, paraphrase, and quotation all bring source material into your writing, but they do different work.

Method What it preserves Typical scope Best used when
Summary A source’s central ideas and overall direction A whole text or substantial section Readers need concise background or an overview
Paraphrase The meaning and detail of a specific passage A sentence or short passage The idea matters more than the original wording
Quotation The source’s exact language A word, phrase, sentence, or carefully selected passage The wording itself is important, precise, revealing, or difficult to reproduce accurately

Ask before borrowing

Before placing source material in a paragraph, ask:

  1. What point am I making?
  2. What does this source contribute to that point?
  3. Does my reader need the whole idea, the detailed idea, or the exact language?
  4. Why is this particular passage more useful than another part of the source?
  5. What will I explain after presenting it?

A quotation should not enter a paragraph simply because it sounds academic or fills space. A paraphrase should not be used merely to disguise copied wording. Every borrowing needs a job.

Key Insight: Choose the source-use method that serves your idea. Do not make the method choose the paragraph for you.

One source, three possible uses

Practice Passage:

Short-form video platforms organize attention through design choices such as autoplay, personalized recommendations, repeated visual patterns, and the absence of a natural stopping point. These features do not determine what every user will do, but they shape the conditions in which users make choices.

Summary:

The passage argues that platform design can influence user attention without fully controlling individual behavior.

Paraphrase of the second sentence:

Although design features do not dictate each person’s actions, they affect the environment in which those decisions occur.

Quotation of a key phrase:

The passage describes design as shaping “the conditions in which users make choices.”

Each version is accurate, but each emphasizes something different.

A direct quotation reproduces a source’s language exactly. Because quotations place another writer’s voice inside your paragraph, they should be selected carefully and used for a clear reason.

When to quote

Quote when:

  • the source’s exact wording is especially distinctive, memorable, concise, or difficult to reproduce without losing meaning;
  • you are analyzing the writer’s diction, tone, imagery, sentence structure, definition, or other language choice;
  • a source provides precise technical, legal, historical, or disciplinary language that must remain exact;
  • an authority’s exact statement matters to your readers more than a general restatement would;
  • you need to present a writer’s position or counterargument fairly before responding to it;
  • a brief quotation would be clearer and more efficient than a longer paraphrase.

The first question is not whether the source has “high ethos.” That phrase is too vague to guide a writing decision. Ask instead: What would readers gain from seeing this writer’s exact words?

When not to quote

Avoid quoting when:

  • the passage states a general fact that can be summarized more efficiently;
  • the wording is ordinary and the idea is easy to paraphrase accurately;
  • the quotation merely repeats the point you have already made;
  • the quotation is being used instead of your own explanation;
  • a long quotation contains only one short phrase that actually matters;
  • you have not yet decided how the quotation supports the paragraph.

Quote only what you need

Writers often copy an entire sentence when only a phrase carries the useful language.

Practice Passage:

Students may experience a course website not simply as a container for assignments but as an environment that quietly directs attention through labels, order, color, and repeated pathways.

Too much quoted material:

The guide explains that “students may experience a course website not simply as a container for assignments but as an environment that quietly directs attention through labels, order, color, and repeated pathways.”

More selective use:

The guide describes a course website as an environment that “quietly directs attention” through its design choices.

The shorter version preserves the phrase worth examining while leaving the student writer in control of the sentence.

Key Insight: Quote the smallest amount of language that preserves what matters.

A quotation should fit grammatically into your writing, identify its source, and include the appropriate MLA citation.

For the examples below, imagine a clearly labeled fictional practice source by Jordan Lee with the quoted material appearing on page 42.

Pattern 1: Name the author in a signal phrase

Lee argues that platform design can “quietly direct attention before a user makes a conscious choice” (42).

Because Lee appears in the sentence, the parenthetical citation contains only the page number.

Pattern 2: Place the author in parentheses

Platform design can “quietly direct attention before a user makes a conscious choice” (Lee 42).

The first element in the parenthetical citation matches the first element of the Works Cited entry.

Do not repeat the author

Incorrect: Lee argues that platform design can “quietly direct attention” (Lee 42).

Correct: Lee argues that platform design can “quietly direct attention” (42).

Three common ways to introduce a quotation

Integrate the quotation into your sentence

No punctuation is needed when the quotation completes the grammar of your sentence:

Lee describes interface design as a force that can “quietly direct attention” (42).

Use a comma after a signal phrase

A comma often follows a signal phrase built around a verb of speaking, writing, or thinking:

Lee warns, “A user does not need to notice a design choice for that choice to shape behavior” (42).

Use a colon after a complete sentence

A colon can introduce a quotation when the words before it form a complete sentence and formally set up what follows:

Lee identifies a central tension in platform design: “Convenience can remove the pauses that allow reflection” (42).

Do not place a colon after a sentence fragment merely because a quotation follows.

Punctuation and the parenthetical citation

For a typical short prose quotation, the closing quotation mark comes before the parenthetical citation, and the sentence period comes after the citation:

Lee calls stopping points “small invitations to reconsider” (47).

A question mark or exclamation point that belongs to the quoted material remains inside the quotation marks. The sentence may still require a parenthetical citation afterward.

Quotations from unpaginated sources

Do not invent a page number or count paragraphs unless the source itself numbers them.

When the author is named in the signal phrase and the online source has no page or other locator, no parenthetical citation is needed:

Mollie Chambers and her coauthors explain that direct quotation preserves a source’s exact language.

When the author is not named in the prose, include the author or shortened title in parentheses:

Direct quotation preserves a source’s exact language (Chambers et al.).

Quotations from videos, podcasts, and speeches

Sources with a runtime can use a timestamp or timestamp range when the exact location will help readers find the material.

Model:

In the video, Long explains that a signal phrase helps identify where borrowed material begins (2:14–28).

The creator’s name or shortened title should correspond to the first element of the Works Cited entry.

Block quotations

In MLA style, a prose quotation that would extend beyond four lines in the paper should normally be formatted as a block quotation.

A block quotation:

  • begins on a new line;
  • is indented one-half inch from the left margin;
  • remains double-spaced;
  • does not use quotation marks around the block;
  • places the parenthetical citation after the final punctuation.

Block quotations should be used sparingly. A longer quotation requires even more preparation and explanation because it occupies more of the paragraph’s territory.

Brackets

Use square brackets to add or visibly alter material needed for clarity or grammar.

Original:

These choices affect the environment before the student begins the task.

Integrated quotation:

Lee argues that “[d]esign choices affect the environment before the student begins the task” (42).

The brackets show that the original capital letter was changed so the quotation would fit the student writer’s sentence.

Brackets may also clarify a pronoun:

Lee observes that “[the recommendation system] continues learning from even brief pauses” (44).

Do not use brackets to rewrite the source’s meaning.

Ellipses

Use an ellipsis to show that words have been omitted from within a quotation.

Lee argues that interface choices “shape the conditions . . . in which users make decisions” (42).

The omission must not distort the source’s grammar, emphasis, or meaning. Do not use an ellipsis merely to stitch together unrelated parts of a source.

Key Insight: Quotation mechanics protect accuracy, but correct punctuation cannot rescue a quotation that has no purpose or explanation.

A paraphrase restates a specific passage in new language and sentence structure while preserving the source’s meaning and level of detail. It still requires citation because the idea came from another source.

When to paraphrase

Paraphrase when:

  • the idea matters more than the writer’s exact wording;
  • you need to explain a specific passage in language that fits your audience and paragraph;
  • the original wording is technical, dense, or stylistically awkward in your context;
  • you want to maintain your own voice and sentence structure;
  • quoting the entire passage would give the source more space than the idea requires.

A reliable paraphrasing process

Step 1: Understand the passage

Identify the central idea, important details, and relationships among those details.

Step 2: Put the source aside

Look away from the wording before drafting. This reduces the temptation to follow the original sentence structure.

Step 3: Explain the idea from memory

Write the meaning as though you were explaining it to a reader who has not seen the source.

Step 4: Change the structure, not only the vocabulary

Reorganize the sentence, combine or divide ideas where appropriate, and choose language that fits your purpose.

Step 5: Compare the paraphrase with the source

Check that you have preserved the meaning, included necessary details, and avoided distinctive wording that should be quoted.

Step 6: Cite the source

A paraphrase uses your language but not your original idea.

Practice Passage

Because many digital feeds remove natural stopping points, users may continue scrolling even after the original purpose of opening the platform has disappeared.

Patchwriting

Because digital feeds take away natural stopping points, people may keep scrolling after their original reason for opening the platform is gone.

This version changes a few words but follows the original structure almost exactly. That is patchwriting, not a fully independent paraphrase.

Effective paraphrase

Once a feed eliminates clear places to pause, a brief visit can continue long after the user has accomplished the task that first brought them to the platform.

The effective version preserves the idea while rebuilding the sentence.

Paraphrase with MLA citation

Using a fictional paginated source by Jordan Lee:

Removing visible stopping points can extend a platform visit beyond the user’s original intention (Lee 53).

Or:

Lee explains that removing visible stopping points can extend a platform visit beyond the user’s original intention (53).

Preserve specialized language when necessary

Some terms cannot or should not be replaced. A paraphrase about mitochondria, due process, confirmation bias, or algorithmic recommendation may need to retain those established terms.

Keeping a necessary technical term is not patchwriting. Copying the source’s distinctive phrasing and sentence structure is.

Paraphrasing does not mean simplifying everything

A paraphrase should preserve meaningful qualifications.

Original:

Platform design can influence attention, although its effects vary according to user habits, purpose, and context.

Misleading paraphrase:

Platform design controls user attention.

Accurate paraphrase:

Interface choices may shape attention, but their effects depend partly on how and why different people use the platform.

The accurate version retains the source’s uncertainty and conditions.

Key Insight: A paraphrase must be independent in language and structure but faithful in meaning.

A signal phrase identifies a source and prepares readers for quoted, paraphrased, or summarized material. It marks the boundary between your voice and the source’s voice.

A signal phrase may include:

  • the author’s name;
  • the title of the work;
  • relevant information about the author or source;
  • a signal verb describing what the source is doing;
  • language showing how the source relates to your point.

Use relevant context, not a miniature biography

Too much:

Jordan Lee, who earned several degrees, has written many articles, teaches at a university, and has appeared at numerous conferences, argues that...

More useful:

Digital-media researcher Jordan Lee argues that...

Include credentials or context only when they help readers evaluate or understand the source.

Signal verbs for different source actions

Source action Useful signal verbs
Makes a claim argues, contends, maintains, asserts, proposes
Explains explains, describes, clarifies, defines, illustrates
Presents evidence reports, finds, demonstrates, documents, observes
Raises uncertainty questions, challenges, complicates, qualifies, cautions
Responds to another view agrees, counters, concedes, disputes, extends
Draws a conclusion concludes, recommends, suggests, warns, predicts

Choose the verb that accurately represents the source. Argues does not mean the same thing as reports. Suggests does not mean the same thing as proves.

Build signal phrases that show relationships

Neutral presentation:

Lee explains that...

Agreement:

Supporting this concern, Lee finds that...

Complication:

Lee complicates this explanation by arguing that...

Contrast:

In contrast to Patel’s emphasis on user choice, Lee maintains that...

Qualification:

Lee agrees that interface design matters but cautions that...

The signal phrase can do more than announce a name. It can begin the work of synthesis and analysis.

Keep source boundaries visible

A paragraph may begin with a signal phrase and then continue for several sentences. Readers still need to know which ideas belong to the source.

Useful boundary markers include:

  • Lee first argues...
  • She then distinguishes...
  • The article next describes...
  • Lee concludes...
  • This evidence suggests...
  • The limitation matters because...

The last two patterns can signal a return to the student writer’s own analysis.

Key Insight: Signal phrases help readers hear the handoff between voices. Signal verbs explain what each voice is doing.

The quote-sandwich method is a useful scaffold for integrating evidence. It helps prevent a quotation from appearing without context or explanation.

The method has three broad moves:

  1. Prepare: establish the point, context, and source.
  2. Present: quote, paraphrase, or summarize the relevant material and cite it.
  3. Explain and connect: interpret the evidence, show why it matters, and reconnect it to the paragraph’s claim.

Although the method is commonly called a quote sandwich, the structure can also help writers integrate paraphrases and brief summaries.

The top layer: Prepare

Before the evidence appears, readers may need:

  • the paragraph’s claim;
  • the source’s identity;
  • relevant context;
  • the relationship between the source and the writer’s idea;
  • a signal phrase and signal verb.

The middle: Present and cite

Select only the material needed for the paragraph. Preserve exact language in quotations, use independent wording in paraphrases, and include the MLA citation.

The lower layer: Explain and connect

After the evidence, answer questions such as:

  • What does this evidence show?
  • Which word, detail, pattern, or qualification matters?
  • How does it support, complicate, or limit my claim?
  • What assumption or consequence should readers notice?
  • How does it connect to the next idea?

A dropped quotation

Using the fictional Jordan Lee practice source:

Platforms can shape user behavior. “Convenience can remove the pauses that allow reflection” (Lee 42).

The quotation is cited, but the paragraph does not explain why the phrase matters.

A developed quotation sandwich

Platform design can influence behavior by changing when users encounter opportunities to stop. Lee warns that “convenience can remove the pauses that allow reflection” (42). The contrast between convenience and reflection is important: a feature may make an action easier while also reducing the moment in which a user might reconsider that action. The quotation therefore supports the larger claim that design shapes behavior partly by controlling the timing of decisions.

The developed version prepares the quotation, presents it accurately, and explains the language and significance.

The sandwich is a scaffold, not a rigid formula

Not every quotation needs exactly one sentence before and one sentence after it. A brief phrase may fit inside a sentence that immediately interprets it. A complex quotation may require several sentences of context and analysis.

The goal is not to produce identical paragraph sandwiches marching across the essay in matching uniforms. The goal is to make the relationship among your claim, the source, and your explanation visible.

Key Insight: Evidence becomes useful when the writer explains its relationship to the paragraph’s purpose.

Source integration is not only a formatting task. It is a responsibility to represent other people’s work accurately and make your own contribution visible.

Quote exactly

Do not silently change spelling, wording, capitalization, or grammar. Use brackets for necessary visible changes and ellipses for omissions that do not distort meaning.

Paraphrase independently

Changing a few words, rearranging phrases, or following the original sentence structure too closely may produce patchwriting.

Cite ideas as well as words

Quotation marks identify exact language. Citations identify the source of both language and ideas. Summaries and paraphrases require citation even though they use your own wording.

Preserve context and qualifications

Do not remove words that limit a claim, reverse its meaning, or make the source appear more certain than it is.

Keep your voice in control

A research paragraph should not become a relay race in which one quotation hands the baton to another while the student writer watches from the bleachers.

Your writing should:

  • establish the paragraph’s point;
  • decide which evidence is necessary;
  • guide readers among sources;
  • explain relationships;
  • draw conclusions.

Common knowledge

Widely known information may not require citation, but the boundary depends on audience and context. When in doubt, cite the source, especially when the information is specific, debatable, statistical, or traceable to a particular writer or study.

Problem 1: The quotation arrives without context

Weak:

“Convenience can remove the pauses that allow reflection” (Lee 42).

Repair: Identify the source and establish why the quotation is entering the paragraph.

Problem 2: The quotation is followed by “This quote shows” but no real explanation

Weak:

This quote shows that design matters.

Repair: Name the specific language or reasoning and explain its significance.

By placing convenience in tension with reflection, Lee shows that an efficient feature may also reduce opportunities for deliberate choice.

Problem 3: The paraphrase is too close to the original

Repair: Put the source aside, explain the idea from memory, rebuild the sentence, then compare for accuracy.

Problem 4: The source is named twice in one citation

Incorrect:

Lee argues that interface design can shape attention (Lee 42).

Correct:

Lee argues that interface design can shape attention (42).

Problem 5: The citation appears, but the source boundary is unclear

Repair: Use signal phrases, pronouns, and transitions to show when the source continues and when your response resumes.

Problem 6: The signal verb exaggerates the source

Weak:

Lee proves that design controls every user.

Repair: Choose a verb and claim that match the source’s actual level of certainty.

Lee suggests that design can influence user behavior under particular conditions.

Problem 7: The quotation is longer than the explanation

A long quotation may overwhelm the paragraph.

Repair: Select a shorter passage or develop enough analysis to justify the quotation’s length.

Problem 8: The writer uses quotation because paraphrasing feels difficult

Difficulty may signal that the passage is not yet understood.

Repair: Define unfamiliar terms, summarize each part, and explain the passage aloud before deciding whether to quote or paraphrase.

Practice 1: Choose the method

For each situation, choose summary, paraphrase, or quotation and explain why:

  • providing two sentences of background from a ten-page article;
  • analyzing a writer’s repeated metaphor;
  • explaining a technical paragraph to a general audience;
  • presenting a counterargument before responding;
  • using one important statistic from a study;
  • discussing the exact language of a policy.

Practice 2: Quote selectively

Here is a practice sentence. Select only the words needed to support a claim about stopping points:

A platform that removes natural stopping points may transform a purposeful visit into an extended session shaped by repetition rather than conscious intention.

Write one sentence that integrates the selected phrase and one sentence that explains it.

Practice 3: Repair the paraphrase

Original:

Repeated recommendations narrow the range of material a user encounters, even when the feed appears to offer endless variety.

Patchwritten version:

Repeated suggestions reduce the range of content a person sees, even though the feed seems to provide unlimited variety.

Rewrite the idea using a genuinely new sentence structure while preserving its meaning.

Practice 4: Signal-verb choices

Choose the most accurate signal verb for each source action:

  1. The writer presents results from a survey.
  2. The writer offers a possible explanation but acknowledges uncertainty.
  3. The writer disputes an earlier interpretation.
  4. The writer defines an unfamiliar term.
  5. The writer recommends a policy change.

Then write a complete signal phrase for each.

Practice 5: MLA citation repair

Correct each sentence:

  1. Lee (2024) argues that platform design shapes attention.
  2. Lee argues that platform design shapes attention (Lee 42).
  3. Platform design shapes attention (Lee, p. 42).
  4. Chambers and her coauthors explain that paraphrase preserves meaning (Chambers et al.). The online source has no page numbers.
  5. Long explains that signal phrases identify sources (Long, 2:14).

Practice 6: Build a complete evidence passage

Use a source from a current assignment and draft:

  1. a sentence stating your paragraph’s point;
  2. a signal phrase establishing the source and its relationship to the point;
  3. a quotation or paraphrase with an MLA citation;
  4. two or more sentences explaining how the evidence supports, complicates, or qualifies the point;
  5. a final sentence connecting the evidence to the paragraph’s larger purpose.

Practice 7: Audit a draft paragraph

Highlight or label:

  • your claim;
  • source context;
  • signal phrase and signal verb;
  • quotation, paraphrase, or summary;
  • MLA citation;
  • explanation;
  • connection to the thesis or next idea.

Any missing element becomes a revision target.

Quotations, paraphrases, and signal phrases help you represent and use sources responsibly. The next chapter turns toward a different question: how do writers make choices that shape an audience’s response?

Before moving forward, choose one source passage and identify:

  1. what the writer is trying to accomplish;
  2. who the likely audience is;
  3. which language or evidence seems designed to influence that audience;
  4. whether quotation or paraphrase would best preserve the feature you want to discuss;
  5. which signal verb accurately describes the writer’s action.
